His work
at Nuenen-Antwerp

Vincent van Gogh's time in both Nuenen and Antwerp played pivotal roles in his artistic journey, shaping his style and influencing his approach to painting. In Nuenen, a small village in the Netherlands, van Gogh resided from 1883 to 1885. It was during this period that he delved into the heart of rural life, immersing himself in the daily routines and struggles of the local farming community. Van Gogh's time in Nuenen allowed him to develop a deep empathy for the working class, and he strove to capture their essence in his art. His paintings from this period, such as "The Potato Eaters," reveal his dedication to portraying the dignity and hardships of ordinary people. In Nuenen, van Gogh's artistic style evolved as he experimented with techniques, honed his use of color and brushwork, and sought to convey the emotions and beauty of everyday life. Following his stay in Nuenen, van Gogh ventured to Antwerp, Belgium, in 1885. Antwerp was known for its vibrant art scene and rich artistic heritage, providing van Gogh with an opportunity to further refine his skills. He enrolled at the Antwerp Academy of Fine Arts, where he studied the works of Old Masters, such as Peter Paul Rubens. In Antwerp, van Gogh was exposed to diverse artistic styles and influences, which expanded his artistic horizons. He experimented with brighter colors, bolder compositions, and a more expressive approach to brushwork. This period of exploration and learning in Antwerp set the stage for the artistic breakthroughs he would later achieve. Both Nuenen and Antwerp offered van Gogh unique environments for artistic growth. Nuenen allowed him to connect deeply with the lives of ordinary people, while Antwerp provided him with the artistic education and exposure to diverse styles that broadened his artistic vision. The experiences and influences from these periods shaped van Gogh's artistic voice, paving the way for the distinct style and emotional depth that would define his later works. The time spent in Nuenen and Antwerp was instrumental in his artistic development, setting the stage for the revolutionary art he would create and the enduring legacy he would leave behind.

Van Gogh had a particular attachment and sympathy for peasants and other working class people that was fueled in several ways. He was particularly fond of the peasant genre work of Jean-François Millet and others. He found the subjects noble and important in the development of modern art. Van Gogh had seen the changing landscape in the Netherlands as industrialization encroached on once pastoral settings and the livelihoods of the working poor with little opportunity to change vocation. Van Gogh had a particular interest in creating character studies of working men and women in the Netherlands and Belgium, such as farmers, weavers, and fishermen. Making up a large body of Van Gogh's work during this period, the character studies were an important, foundational component in his artistic development.
